# ADR 009 — Secondary logo direction: "The Atelier Mark"

**Date:** 2026-04-29
**Status:** **Deferred (2026-04-29)** — Anna-Liv chose to refine the primary Stitched Mark direction (see ADR 006 v2, multi-stitch refinement) rather than pursue this. Kept on file as a fallback / future supporting visual language.
**Source:** Anna-Liv, Session 2

> *"I think just the recycle symbol isn't enough; we need to add some sort of design element. I'm thinking like a fashion designer's brush strokes on a page, sort of like their style guide, so that the triangle is kind of like the triangle for a female symbol, but then maybe a head and some designer-type brush strokes."*

This brief captures that direction so Anna-Liv can play with it in an image generator.

---

## Concept name

**"The Atelier Mark"** — fashion-designer brush-strokes meets recycle loop.

## The construction

A stylised mark drawn as if by a fashion designer's hand. Three gestural brush-strokes loop into one another — forming both **a recycle symbol** *and* a **gestural fashion-sketch frame**. Inside the loop sits an abstract figure: a triangle (suggesting a dress or body) with a small circle above (suggesting a head) — echoing the female symbol but stylised and ambiguous.

## Reference cluster

- **Antonio Lopez** — gestural fashion illustrations
- **René Gruau** — confident brush-and-ink fashion plates
- **Christian Dior atelier sketches** — couture working drawings
- **Coco Chanel sketches** — minimalist atelier shorthand
- **Hermès equestrian hand-illustrations** — luxury craft as brand language

Anti-references: Adobe Stock fashion silhouettes, women's-clothing-store mascots, generic "lady-in-dress" icons, anything that looks like a hen-do invitation.

## Three sub-variants worth testing

### Variant A — The Atelier Figure *(closest to Anna-Liv's description)*
- Centre: triangle (dress) + small circle (head), abstract female figure
- Around it: three flowing ink brush-strokes that loop into a recycle symbol
- Forest-green ink on cream linen paper
- Hand-drawn, confident, slightly imperfect

### Variant B — Brush-strokes Only *(most universal)*
- Three flowing ink brush-strokes, looping into a recycle symbol
- No figure
- Reads as both fashion gestural mark and recycle loop
- No gendering — works for everyone

### Variant C — Figure-Only Fashion Plate *(most distinctive, riskiest)*
- A single elegant figure in a flowing garment, drawn in 3–5 brush-strokes
- The garment's curving line subtly implies a recycle loop
- Most "fashion brand" feeling
- Hardest to scale to small icon sizes

## Trade-offs

| Pro | Con |
|---|---|
| More distinctive than abstract recycle symbol | Harder to scale to 32px favicon (brush detail drops out) |
| Connects directly to fashion-design heritage | Risks dating faster than Stitched Mark (atelier aesthetics cycle) |
| Carries personality at every touchpoint | Variants A and C lean feminine — exclude Sarah persona slightly |
| Pinterest-y — matches Anna-Liv's experiential reference | Risks reading as "boutique fashion blog" not "tech-forward eco platform" |

---

## **Strategic synthesis option (recommended)**

Don't make this the *primary* logo. **Use both:**

1. **Primary mark = Stitched Mark** (ADR 005/006) — the embroidered recycle symbol on linen. Refined, scales, ages well. The thing that goes on the app icon, favicon, business card, social avatar.

2. **Supporting visual language = Atelier brush-strokes** — used throughout the brand for:
   - Page decorations and section dividers
   - Hero illustrations (a brush-stroke figure beside a manifesto)
   - Social post graphics
   - Onboarding screen illustrations
   - "Did You Know" feed cards
   - Empty states ("your wardrobe is empty — let's draw the first piece in")

This is exactly how **Hermès** pairs their classical horse-and-carriage logo with hand-drawn equestrian illustrations, or how **Loewe** pairs their refined wordmark with hand-painted craft campaigns. You get the discipline of a refined logo *and* the personality of fashion-illustration energy in the brand experience.

**Why this beats picking one:**
- Logo doesn't need to do all the brand's emotional work alone
- Primary mark stays scalable and timeless
- Brush-stroke aesthetic gets to live everywhere it's most useful (storytelling, illustration, marketing) without being constrained by logo-scaling rules
- Anna-Liv gets *both* design moves she's drawn to (textile craft + drawing craft) at the same time
